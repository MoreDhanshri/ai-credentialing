# Credentialing in payer and managed care organizations

**Credentialing is the systematic process health plans use to verify that every provider in their network is qualified, licensed, and safe to treat members—and it remains one of the most labor-intensive, error-prone, and costly administrative functions in U.S. healthcare.** The process costs the industry an estimated **$2.1 billion annually**, takes 90–120 days per provider on average, and directly fuels the ghost network crisis that leaves millions of patients unable to access care. With NCQA's tightened 2025 standards now requiring monthly monitoring and compressed verification windows, payers face a stark choice: automate or drown in manual work. This report covers the full credentialing landscape from the health plan perspective—definitions, regulatory requirements, workflows, costs, pain points, and emerging technology solutions.

---

## What credentialing is and why every health plan must do it

Credentialing is a health plan's formal evaluation and verification of a provider's qualifications before granting network participation. The sequence runs **credentialing → contracting → enrollment → billing activation**. It is distinct from Medicare provider enrollment (via PECOS), which registers providers for government reimbursement. Health plans credential to protect patient safety, limit malpractice liability, comply with federal and state law, prevent fraud, and maintain network quality.

Three types exist. **Provider credentialing** applies to individual practitioners—physicians, NPs, PAs, behavioral health clinicians, dentists, and others permitted to practice independently under state law. **Facility/organizational credentialing** covers hospitals, ASCs, SNFs, labs, home health agencies, and other institutional providers, requiring verification of state licensure, accreditation status, and Medicare/Medicaid certification. **Delegated credentialing** occurs when a health plan contractually authorizes a hospital system, large medical group, IPA, or Credentials Verification Organization (CVO) to perform credentialing on the plan's behalf, though the plan retains ultimate legal and regulatory accountability.

The consequences of doing it badly are severe. Payments to excluded providers can trigger **False Claims Act treble damages** and OIG Civil Monetary Penalties of **$100,000 per arrangement** plus **$10,000 per item or service**. Plans risk loss of NCQA accreditation, CMS contract termination, state enforcement actions, and negligent credentialing lawsuits if an unqualified provider harms a member.

## The credentialing workflow from application to ongoing monitoring

The process begins when a provider submits credentials through **CAQH ProView**, the industry-standard platform used by **2.5 million providers** and accepted by over **1,000 health plans** in all 50 states. Providers must re-attest to data accuracy every 120 days. Credentialing staff pull the application, review it for completeness, and begin primary source verification—the core of the entire process.

**Primary source verification (PSV)** requires confirming approximately 14 data elements directly with the issuing authority, not from the provider's self-report. The standard verification set includes:

| Data element | Primary source |
|---|---|
| State licensure (current, active) | State licensing board |
| Medical education | Medical school, AMA Physician Masterfile, or AOA |
| Residency/fellowship training | Training program or AMA Masterfile |
| Board certification (if claimed) | ABMS CertiFACTS or specialty board |
| DEA registration | DEA database |
| Malpractice insurance | Insurance carrier or face sheet |
| Malpractice claims history (5 years) | NPDB query and carrier |
| Work history (5 years) | Self-reported; gaps >6 months require explanation |
| Hospital privileges | Hospital verification |
| OIG/LEIE exclusion | HHS OIG List of Excluded Individuals/Entities |
| SAM exclusion | GSA SAM.gov |
| NPDB query | National Practitioner Data Bank |
| Medicare/Medicaid sanctions | CMS, state Medicaid exclusion lists |
| State licensing sanctions | State licensing board |

The AMA Physician Masterfile covers **1.4 million physicians** and serves as NCQA-accepted primary source for education, training, and board certification. The ABMS database covers **997,000+ physicians** with daily certification updates. Approximately **42 states** maintain their own Medicaid exclusion lists separate from the federal OIG list.

Once PSV is complete, the file goes to a **credentialing committee**—a peer review body chaired by a medical director with physician representation. "Clean" files may be approved on a rolling basis by the committee chair; files with issues (malpractice history, sanctions, gaps) require full committee discussion. Decisions include approve, approve with conditions, deny, or defer. Denied providers have **appeal and fair hearing rights** under both NCQA standards and 42 CFR §422.202(d).

After approval, the provider enters contracting and enrollment. NCQA 2025 standards require notification within **30 calendar days** of the decision. Between credentialing cycles, health plans must conduct **ongoing monitoring**: monthly OIG/LEIE and SAM checks, monthly license expiration monitoring (new in 2025), and NPDB Continuous Query for automatic alerts. Recredentialing must occur at least every **36 months**.

NCQA also permits **provisional credentialing**—a one-time, 60-day fast track requiring only license verification, five years of malpractice history, and a signed attestation.

## Timelines stretch to months, costing providers thousands daily

Initial credentialing for commercial payers typically takes **90–120 days**, though complex or multi-state cases can exceed 180 days. Medicare enrollment through PECOS runs **30–65 days** (electronic vs. paper). Medicaid credentialing varies by state from **45 to 90 days** but can stretch to 6–12 months. Recredentialing is generally faster at **60–120 days**, since baseline data already exists.

Under NCQA's updated 2025 standards (effective July 1, 2025), PSV must be completed within **120 days** of the credentialing decision for accredited health plans (reduced from 180 days) and within **90 days** for certified CVOs. NCQA originally proposed 90 days for accredited plans but revised to 120 after industry pushback.

The dominant bottleneck is **application quality**: an estimated **85% of credentialing applications contain errors or missing information** on first submission. Other delays include unresponsive primary sources, monthly-only committee meeting schedules, expired CAQH attestations, payer-specific form requirements, and data mismatches across systems. The average physician contracts with **20+ private payers**, each requiring separate credentialing with distinct processes.

The financial toll on providers is substantial. Revenue loss during credentialing delays runs **$1,000–$5,000 per provider per day**—meaning a 90-day delay can cost a specialist **$450,000–$1.2 million** in unrecoverable revenue. **54% of medical practices** reported increased credentialing-related claim denials in 2021 (MGMA), and **one in three billing denials** trace to credentialing errors. The average cost to rework a denied claim is **$25–$181**, and **$262 billion** in annual provider revenue is lost to claim denials industry-wide.

## Manual labor requirements remain stubbornly high

Without automation, a credentialing specialist processes roughly **80 provider packets per month**. With modern software, that jumps to approximately **250 per month**—a 3x improvement. For a 10,000-provider network generating approximately 3,833 credentialing events annually (recredentialing plus new adds), a health plan needs **4 FTEs without automation or 2 FTEs with it**. A medium-sized hospital typically employs **3–5 full-time credentialing specialists**. Average specialist salary runs **$52,306** (ZipRecruiter), with fully loaded costs of **$50,000–$80,000** per FTE.

The most labor-intensive steps are information gathering and PSV (contacting schools, boards, and agencies), data entry across multiple systems, follow-up on incomplete applications, and committee packet preparation. Each application consumes approximately **20+ hours of staff time**. Yet **52% of credentialing teams** report their workflows are **entirely manual** (Medallion 2024 survey), and **84%** experience turnaround times exceeding 15 days.

The total cost per credentialing event, including staff time, technology, verification fees, and overhead, is estimated at **$7,000–$8,000 per provider**. Across the industry, health plans and provider organizations spend an estimated **$2.1 billion annually** on credentialing activities, with larger health systems running into the millions per year.

## A dense regulatory framework governs every step

### CMS requirements form the federal floor

For Medicare Advantage, **42 CFR §422.204** is the foundational regulation. It mandates written credentialing policies, PSV of licensure and certification, NPDB queries, disciplinary status checks, eligibility verification for Medicare payment, and **recredentialing at least every three years** with consideration of performance indicators including quality data, utilization, grievances, and member satisfaction. The MA organization must have a process for consulting with contracting professionals about criteria, and **§422.205** prohibits discrimination against providers solely based on license type. Under **§422.202(d)**, providers must receive written notice of adverse decisions and a right to appeal.

For Medicaid managed care, **42 CFR §438.214** requires states to ensure MCOs implement written credentialing and recredentialing policies. Related sections—§438.206 (network adequacy), §438.68 (quantitative network standards), and §438.12 (anti-discrimination)—create an interlocking framework. **§438.230** governs delegation of credentialing to subcontractors.

### NCQA standards dominate accreditation

NCQA accredits over **1,000 health plan products** covering approximately **173 million enrollees**. Many states mandate NCQA accreditation for Medicaid managed care participation. The CR domain contains nine standards:

- **CR 1** (Policies): Credentialing guidelines, practitioner rights, system controls
- **CR 2** (Committee): Physician-led credentialing committee
- **CR 3** (Verification): PSV of all 14 standard elements
- **CR 4** (Cycle length): Recredentialing every 36 months
- **CR 5** (Ongoing monitoring): Between-cycle sanctions and quality monitoring
- **CR 6** (Reporting/appeals): Reporting to authorities; practitioner appeal rights
- **CR 7** (Organizational providers): Facility assessment processes
- **CR 8** (Information integrity): New in 2025—audit trails, staff training, compliance audits
- **CR 9** (Delegation): Written agreements, pre-delegation audits, annual oversight

Key 2025 updates include compressed verification windows (180→120 days), mandatory Medicare/Medicaid exclusion verification, monthly license monitoring, 30-day notification windows, and race/ethnicity/language data collection fields on applications.

### URAC and state laws add additional layers

URAC, recognized in **13 states**, offers alternative accreditation with similar PSV and recredentialing requirements but emphasizes consumer protection over clinical performance metrics. The Joint Commission accredits managed care organizations but holds a much smaller market share than NCQA for health plan credentialing.

State prompt credentialing laws create binding timelines. California's **AB 1041** (effective January 2027) mandates credentialing decisions within **90 days** (60 days for behavioral health), with provisional approval if the deadline is missed and criminal penalties for violations. Illinois requires **60-day** credentialing completion. Texas mandates use of a standardized credentialing application. The **No Surprises Act** requires directory verification every 90 days and updates within **2 business days** of receiving new provider information, with penalties up to **$100 per day per affected individual** for plan violations.

## Delegated credentialing shifts work but not accountability

In delegated credentialing, a health plan contractually authorizes a hospital system, medical group, IPA, or CVO to perform credentialing functions. The delegation sequence begins with a **readiness assessment**, followed by **pre-delegation file audit** (typically reviewing 5–30 provider files), execution of a binding delegation agreement, and operational launch with ongoing oversight.

Delegation agreements must specify scope of work, turnaround requirements, reporting obligations, audit rights, corrective action procedures, and the payer's **final decision authority** on provider participation. Under NCQA CR 9, plans must conduct **annual audits** and periodic performance assessments of delegates. Organizations with NCQA Credentialing Accreditation receive reduced audit requirements. NCQA prohibits plans from delegating more than **50% of credentialing decision-making** while retaining CR Accreditation eligibility.

For health plans, delegation reduces administrative burden, enables faster network expansion, and leverages specialized expertise. The risks are loss of direct control, continued regulatory accountability, and reliance on delegates for data accuracy feeding provider directories. For delegated entities, the trade-off is faster provider onboarding and earlier reimbursement against increased liability, compliance investment, and audit burden.

Credentialing is one of the **most commonly delegated functions** alongside utilization management, with the trend accelerating as payer backlogs and staffing shortages worsen—**57% of credentialing teams** reported turnover and staffing challenges in a 2024 survey. Over **150 organizations** currently hold NCQA Credentialing Accreditation, and most large health systems with 150+ providers pursue delegated status.

## Ghost networks expose credentialing's downstream failures

Ghost networks—directories listing providers who are unavailable, unreachable, or not actually participating—represent the most visible failure of the credentialing and provider data management ecosystem. A **JAMA Open Network study** found **81% of entries** in directories of five large insurers contained inaccuracies. The Senate Finance Committee's 2023 secret shopper study across 12 MA plans revealed **33% of phone numbers were inaccurate**, appointments could be made only **18% of the time**, and **over 80% of listed mental health providers were ghosts**.

University of Maryland research analyzing 432,146 providers across 3,659 MA plans found **nearly 40%** were likely ghosts (billing fewer than 11 MA beneficiaries). A Wall Street Journal investigation showed more than **one-third of doctors** listed in Medicaid networks across 22 states didn't actually serve Medicaid patients.

Regulatory response is intensifying. The **REAL Health Providers Act**, signed into law **February 3, 2026**, requires MA plans to conduct annual directory updates, accuracy analyses, and publish accuracy scores starting in 2028. CMS now requires MA plans to submit directory information to Medicare Plan Finder and annually verify that listed behavioral health providers served at least **20 patients**. California's attorney general secured a **$40 million settlement** with Health Net (Centene) over inaccurate directories in October 2025. Cigna settled ghost network claims for **$5.7 million**, and a wave of class actions in 2026 targets EmblemHealth, Blue Shield of California, and others.

Ghost networks trace directly to credentialing failures: stale data between recredentialing cycles, delegated entities failing to maintain rosters, lack of real-time data exchange, and the basic reality that credentialing databases become outdated quickly. Provider directory maintenance costs **$2.76 billion annually** across physician practices (CAQH), and the fragmented, payer-by-payer approach to data collection undermines accuracy at every turn.

## Technology is compressing timelines from months to days

The credentialing software market was valued at **$807.8 million in 2023** and is projected to reach **$1.42 billion by 2030** (8.3% CAGR). The broader CVO services market stood at approximately **$1.65 billion in 2024**, projected to reach **$3.86 billion by 2033**. Cloud-based solutions dominate with **68% market share**, and the healthcare payers segment is growing fastest.

The vendor landscape spans legacy leaders and AI-native startups. **symplr** (incorporating Cactus Software) leads the market with 3,500+ healthcare organization clients, 700+ credentialing specialists, and NCQA certification across all 11 verification areas. **HealthStream/VerityStream** offers CredentialStream with connections to approximately **1,500 databases** for real-time PSV. **Medallion**, backed by $85 million from Sequoia and Google Ventures, claims **78% faster** credentialing with **99.5% accuracy** and launched a one-day credentialing solution in February 2024. **Assured**, founded in 2024, credentials providers in **2 business days** across 2,000+ primary sources. **CertifyOS** offers an API-first platform estimating automated credentialing could save the industry **$25 billion annually**. **Sutherland's SmartCred** reports **64% cost reduction** and cut credentialing from 5 days to 2 for a Fortune 500 insurer.

Nearly every manual step now has an automation pathway. AI-powered OCR and NLP extract data from licenses and certificates, eliminating up to **70% of manual data entry**. Automated PSV engines query hundreds of primary sources simultaneously, returning results in seconds rather than weeks. RPA bots handle CAQH data ingestion, sanctions screening, license tracking, and enrollment form population. Predictive analytics identify error-prone applications and slow payers before they create bottlenecks.

The shift to **continuous monitoring** makes automation virtually mandatory. NCQA's 2025 standards require monthly screening of every provider for license expirations, OIG exclusions, and Medicare/Medicaid sanctions—impossible to do manually across networks of tens of thousands of providers. More than **50% of health plans** now use AI tools in administrative workflows (2025 CAQH Index), and the industry avoided **$258 billion** in administrative costs in 2024 through electronic transactions. Yet the automation savings opportunity remains enormous: CAQH estimates **$20–21 billion** in additional savings from fully transitioning remaining manual workflows.

Emerging approaches include **multi-payer shared credentialing** (Medallion's CredAlliance, Sutherland's multi-payer model), where providers are verified once and data is shared across participating plans—potentially cutting costs by 50% and reducing provider abrasion. FHIR-based interoperability is advancing ahead of January 2027 CMS requirements, with CAQH building an Endpoint Directory of validated payer FHIR endpoints. Blockchain credentialing remains in pilot stage—UnitedHealthcare, Optum, Humana, and Quest Diagnostics launched a blockchain directory pilot in 2018—but is expected to reach meaningful adoption within **5–7 years**. Credentialing-as-a-Service (CaaS) models are becoming the dominant delivery approach for payers seeking scale without additional headcount.

## Conclusion

Credentialing in managed care sits at a critical inflection point. The function remains anchored by dense regulation—42 CFR §422.204 for Medicare Advantage, §438.214 for Medicaid, nine NCQA CR standards, state prompt credentialing laws, and the No Surprises Act—creating a compliance environment that punishes shortcuts. Yet the operational reality is that **85% of applications arrive with errors**, half of all credentialing teams work entirely manually, and the average process takes three to four months while costing providers thousands of dollars per day in lost revenue.

The ghost network crisis has elevated credentialing from a back-office function to a regulatory flashpoint, with the REAL Health Providers Act, state attorney general settlements, and a wave of class-action litigation forcing health plans to rethink how they manage provider data. NCQA's compressed 2025 timelines and mandatory monthly monitoring make the status quo untenable for any plan managing a large network without automation.

The most consequential shift is the emergence of AI-native platforms capable of compressing credentialing from months to days while achieving **99%+ accuracy**, combined with multi-payer shared credentialing models that attack the fundamental inefficiency of duplicate verification across 20+ payers per provider. Health plans that invest in these capabilities will build faster networks, reduce provider abrasion, and avoid the regulatory and reputational costs that are now reaching tens of millions of dollars per enforcement action. Those that don't will face growing backlogs, accelerating staff turnover, and the compounding legal exposure of inaccurate provider directories.
